Cellular Respiration and Energy Production

Cellular respiration is the process by which cells break down glucose to produce adenosine triphosphate (ATP), the primary energy currency of the cell. This metabolic pathway occurs in three main stages: glycolysis, the citric acid cycle (Krebs cycle), and oxidative phosphorylation. Understanding these stages is fundamental to grasping how living organisms convert the chemical energy stored in food into a usable form that powers everything from muscle contraction to nerve impulse transmission.

Glycolysis takes place in the cytoplasm and does not require oxygen, making it an anaerobic process. During glycolysis, one molecule of glucose (a six-carbon sugar) is split into two molecules of pyruvate (three-carbon compounds). This process produces a net gain of 2 ATP molecules and 2 NADH molecules. The ten enzymatic steps of glycolysis are among the most ancient metabolic pathways, found in virtually all living organisms from bacteria to humans. This universality suggests that glycolysis evolved very early in the history of life, likely before the atmosphere contained significant amounts of oxygen. The regulation of glycolysis is tightly controlled by three key enzymes: hexokinase, phosphofructokinase (PFK), and pyruvate kinase. Of these, PFK is the primary regulatory point, inhibited by high levels of ATP and citrate, and activated by AMP and ADP. This ensures that glycolysis speeds up when energy is needed and slows down when the cell has sufficient ATP.

The citric acid cycle occurs in the mitochondrial matrix. Before entering the cycle, pyruvate is converted to acetyl-CoA by the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex, releasing one CO2 molecule and generating one NADH. This irreversible reaction commits the carbon atoms to complete oxidation. Acetyl-CoA then enters the citric acid cycle by combining with oxaloacetate to form citrate, a six-carbon molecule. Through a series of eight enzymatic reactions, citrate is systematically oxidized back to oxaloacetate, completing the cycle. Each turn of the cycle produces 3 NADH, 1 FADH2, 1 GTP (equivalent to ATP), and 2 CO2 molecules. Since two pyruvate molecules are produced per glucose, the cycle turns twice per glucose molecule. The CO2 released during the citric acid cycle is the same CO2 that we exhale — it represents the complete oxidation of the carbon atoms that were originally part of the glucose molecule. The citric acid cycle also serves as a metabolic hub, providing precursors for amino acid synthesis, fatty acid synthesis, and gluconeogenesis.

Oxidative phosphorylation is the final and most productive stage, taking place in the inner mitochondrial membrane. The electron transport chain (ETC) accepts electrons from NADH and FADH2, passing them through a series of four protein complexes. Complex I (NADH dehydrogenase) accepts electrons from NADH, while Complex II (succinate dehydrogenase) accepts electrons from FADH2. Electrons then flow to Complex III (cytochrome bc1) via the mobile carrier ubiquinone, and finally to Complex IV (cytochrome c oxidase) via cytochrome c. At Complex IV, electrons are transferred to molecular oxygen, the final electron acceptor, forming water. As electrons flow through Complexes I, III, and IV, the energy released is used to pump protons from the mitochondrial matrix into the intermembrane space, creating an electrochemical gradient known as the proton motive force. This gradient represents stored potential energy. ATP synthase, sometimes called Complex V, acts as a molecular turbine — protons flow back through this enzyme down their concentration gradient, and the rotational energy drives the synthesis of ATP from ADP and inorganic phosphate. This process is called chemiosmosis, a term coined by Peter Mitchell, who received the Nobel Prize for this discovery in 1978. Approximately 34 ATP molecules are produced during oxidative phosphorylation.

In total, the complete oxidation of one glucose molecule can yield approximately 36-38 ATP molecules, though the exact number varies depending on the shuttle system used to transport cytoplasmic NADH into the mitochondria. The malate-aspartate shuttle yields 2.5 ATP per NADH, while the less efficient glycerol-3-phosphate shuttle yields only 1.5 ATP. This remarkable efficiency — capturing roughly 34% of the total energy available in glucose — makes aerobic respiration far more productive than anaerobic alternatives like fermentation, which yields only 2 ATP per glucose. Cells that lack mitochondria or operate under low-oxygen conditions must rely on fermentation, producing either ethanol (in yeast) or lactate (in human muscle cells during intense exercise) as byproducts.
