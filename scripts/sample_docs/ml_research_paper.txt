Efficient Knowledge Distillation for Edge Deployment of Large Language Models

Abstract: The deployment of large language models (LLMs) on edge devices remains challenging due to memory and computational constraints. This paper presents EdgeDistill, a novel knowledge distillation framework that transfers capabilities from a 7B-parameter teacher model to a 500M-parameter student model while retaining 89% of the teacher's performance on standard NLP benchmarks. Our approach introduces three key innovations: layer-selective attention transfer, task-adaptive temperature scheduling, and vocabulary pruning based on domain frequency analysis.

Introduction: As LLMs become increasingly capable, the gap between their computational requirements and the resources available on edge devices continues to widen. A GPT-class model with 7B parameters requires approximately 14GB of memory in half-precision, far exceeding the capabilities of most mobile and IoT devices. Knowledge distillation offers a promising path to bridge this gap by training smaller student models to mimic the behavior of larger teacher models.

Methodology: We trained our student model using a three-phase distillation process. Phase 1 (Logit Matching): The student is trained to match the softmax output distribution of the teacher across 500K examples from the C4 dataset, using KL divergence loss with temperature T=4. Phase 2 (Attention Transfer): We selectively transfer attention patterns from layers 1, 6, 12, and 18 of the teacher to corresponding layers in the student, using mean squared error loss on the attention matrices. Phase 3 (Task Fine-tuning): The distilled student is fine-tuned on downstream tasks using a combined loss of cross-entropy and continued KL divergence from the teacher (alpha=0.3).

Results: EdgeDistill achieved an average score of 78.3% across GLUE benchmark tasks, compared to 88.1% for the teacher model and 71.2% for a student trained from scratch. On the SQuAD 2.0 reading comprehension task, our model achieved an F1 score of 82.7 compared to the teacher's 91.3. Inference latency on an ARM Cortex-A78 processor was 47ms per token, a 14x improvement over the teacher model running on the same hardware. Memory footprint was reduced to 1.1GB from 14GB.

Limitations: Performance degradation is most pronounced on tasks requiring complex multi-step reasoning, where the student model's smaller capacity becomes a bottleneck. Additionally, the distillation process itself requires access to the full teacher model and significant computational resources for training.
