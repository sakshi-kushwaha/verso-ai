Efficient Knowledge Distillation for Edge Deployment of Large Language Models

Abstract: The deployment of large language models (LLMs) on edge devices remains challenging due to memory and computational constraints. This paper presents EdgeDistill, a novel knowledge distillation framework that transfers capabilities from a 7B-parameter teacher model to a 500M-parameter student model while retaining 89% of the teacher's performance on standard NLP benchmarks. Our approach introduces three key innovations: layer-selective attention transfer, task-adaptive temperature scheduling, and vocabulary pruning based on domain frequency analysis. We evaluate EdgeDistill on seven benchmark datasets and demonstrate consistent improvements over existing distillation methods, with inference latency reduced by 14x on ARM-based edge hardware.

Introduction: As LLMs become increasingly capable, the gap between their computational requirements and the resources available on edge devices continues to widen. A GPT-class model with 7B parameters requires approximately 14GB of memory in half-precision floating point, far exceeding the capabilities of most mobile and IoT devices. Knowledge distillation offers a promising path to bridge this gap by training smaller student models to mimic the behavior of larger teacher models. However, traditional distillation approaches face several challenges when applied to modern LLMs. First, the massive vocabulary size (typically 32K-100K tokens) creates an extremely high-dimensional output space for the student to match. Second, the deep attention patterns in transformer models encode hierarchical linguistic knowledge that shallow students cannot easily replicate. Third, uniform temperature scaling across all training examples fails to account for varying difficulty levels across different linguistic tasks and domains.

Prior work in knowledge distillation has explored logit matching, feature matching, and attention transfer as separate techniques. DistilBERT demonstrated that distilling BERT into a 6-layer model could retain 97% of performance, but this approach does not scale to autoregressive LLMs with much larger parameter gaps. TinyBERT introduced multi-layer attention transfer but required the student architecture to mirror the teacher's structure. MiniLM proposed distilling self-attention relations rather than raw attention weights, achieving better cross-architecture transfer. Our work builds on these foundations while addressing the unique challenges of distilling billion-parameter autoregressive models into sub-billion student models suitable for edge deployment.

Methodology: We trained our student model using a three-phase distillation process designed to progressively transfer different aspects of the teacher's knowledge. Phase 1 (Logit Matching): The student is trained to match the softmax output distribution of the teacher across 500K examples from the C4 dataset, using KL divergence loss with a task-adaptive temperature schedule. Rather than using a fixed temperature T, we introduce an adaptive schedule where T starts at 6.0 for the first 20% of training (encouraging broader distribution matching), decreases linearly to 2.0 during the middle 60%, and remains at 2.0 for the final 20% (focusing on sharp prediction accuracy). Phase 2 (Attention Transfer): We selectively transfer attention patterns from layers 1, 6, 12, and 18 of the teacher to corresponding layers in the 8-layer student, using mean squared error loss on the attention matrices. Layer selection was determined through preliminary experiments measuring attention pattern diversity — we chose layers with maximally different attention distributions to ensure diverse knowledge transfer. Phase 3 (Task Fine-tuning): The distilled student is fine-tuned on downstream tasks using a combined loss of cross-entropy and continued KL divergence from the teacher with alpha=0.3. This continued distillation during fine-tuning prevents catastrophic forgetting of the general knowledge acquired in Phases 1 and 2.

Our vocabulary pruning strategy analyzes token frequency distributions across domain-specific corpora and removes tokens that appear fewer than 100 times across all training data. This reduced the vocabulary from 32,000 to 24,500 tokens, yielding a 12% reduction in embedding layer parameters without measurable performance degradation on our target domains.

Results: EdgeDistill achieved an average score of 78.3% across GLUE benchmark tasks, compared to 88.1% for the teacher model and 71.2% for a student trained from scratch. This represents an 89% performance retention rate with a 14x parameter reduction. On the SQuAD 2.0 reading comprehension task, our model achieved an F1 score of 82.7 compared to the teacher's 91.3. On the XSUM summarization task, ROUGE-L was 31.2 versus the teacher's 38.7. Inference latency on an ARM Cortex-A78 processor was 47ms per token, a 14x improvement over the teacher model running on the same hardware. Memory footprint was reduced to 1.1GB from 14GB, comfortably fitting within the 2GB RAM budget of most modern smartphones. Battery consumption during continuous inference was measured at 0.8W compared to the teacher model's estimated 12W on equivalent GPU hardware.

Limitations and Future Work: Performance degradation is most pronounced on tasks requiring complex multi-step reasoning, where the student model's smaller capacity becomes a bottleneck. On the GSM8K mathematical reasoning benchmark, our student retained only 61% of the teacher's performance. Additionally, the distillation process itself requires access to the full teacher model and significant computational resources for training — approximately 200 GPU-hours on A100 hardware. Future work will explore progressive distillation chains, where intermediate-sized models serve as stepping stones between the teacher and the final edge-deployed student.
